Then and Now


An autumn hike through the appalachian mountains: the sweet scent of damp fallen leaves, an owl’s call echoing through the hills, tree colors, inexplicable in their vibrant fullness. These mountains have been a refuge for thousands, hiking, camping, hunting, and fishing amidst their natural beauty; they’ve also been a home for many. Mike Maynor, a father of two, has lived in these mountains since he was a child, but in a few short moments, the orange autumn mountaintop of millions of years was gone - destroyed in an instantaneous burst of smoke and earth. A black cloud of dust rolled down the side of this mountain, this place of peace, past his home as it settled to reveal the coal underneath. Mountaintop Removal Mining to attain coal as a resource is a process through which hundreds of feet of earth are exploded from the top of mountains to more easily access to the coal resources that lie underneath. In the process, the natural beauty of the land is demolished forever, streams are destroyed, valleys are filled, and water and air is polluted, not to mention the release of CO2 into the atmosphere that will occur as a result of these materials. Through this process, coal mining companies have destroyed nearly one million acres of appalachian forest. The earth we’ve been given is full of priceless wonders, wonders given to man by God. God wants us to breathe in his wonders, to be filled by them, to find peace in them, for them to be tools to renew our faith in him; we cannot destroy these gifts, and we must stop.

Notes: Track 2 Psalm: Psalm 8

All the things of this world bear the marks of God their Creator and sustainer. While we hold a special place in that creation, we are still a part of the created order, and like the Psalmist, we can look to the rest of creation with wonder and awe, seeing God and the signs of his work present in them.

Notes: Mark 10:2-16

God created the world with a certain order of things in mind. While here he discusses it in terms of husband and wife, the larger statement is that God means for us to be in relationship and interdependence with other created beings, and to be a part of the created order, not its master. That the world is larger than we are is conveyed also in the call to a childlike wonder at and reception of the Kingdom of God. Children have a profound way of being amazed at even the small things of the world, appreciating them for what they are, not how they can be used.

Notes: Psalm 24

This is the psalm for the entrance to the temple: that, as one enters into the place of worship, the mind is turned to God’s rule of the earth and all its creatures, including humans. I wonder what our worship would look like today if we began it by looking out at the world and remembering that it does not belong to us?

Notes: Psalm 146

This is a song of praise – and what is it that drives the psalmist to praise? God the creator, and God the protector of the oppressed and poor, against those who work against them. God as creator and God as protector of the poor are clearly linked – both point to God as looking at the things God has made, and saying – I love this earth and its people. How can we look at what God has made and do the same?

Notes: Psalm 93

Listen to the floods lift up their voice! Here we are reminded that the world around is God’s creation, and creation echoes that in its worship of the divine. How would we treat creation differently if we considered that creation itself is worshipping God?
