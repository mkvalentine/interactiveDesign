There was once a man in the land of Uz whose name was Job. That man was blameless and upright, one who feared God and turned away from evil.

One day the heavenly beings came to present themselves before the LORD, and Satan also came among them to present himself before the LORD. The LORD said to Satan, "Where have you come from?" Satan answered the LORD, "From going to and fro on the earth, and from walking up and down on it." The LORD said to Satan, "Have you considered my servant Job? The LORD said to Satan, "Very well, he is in your power; only spare his life."

Kaya, an adorable 11 year old child who hopes to be a doctor when she grows up, lives in Bangladesh with her family of six. During the rainy season, she and her family are forced to live in a hut raised on wooden platforms. They bathe in this disgusting water, live in raised huts for up to a month at a time, and she and her siblings had to swim through this water in order to get to school. The house in which her family used to live now is in the middle of a river. Kaya’s story is not unique, and the results of climate change are affecting children all over the world, especially in the poorest countries. Furthermore, this will only increase as further climate change contributes to more droughts, forest fires, hurricanes, and foods. These innocent children do not have any choice in where they are born nor do they have any control over the decisions that we make that inadvertently affect them. We, in the United States, emit around 20% of the world’s greenhouse gas emissions; these emissions lead to the warming of our planet and the flooding of some of the world’s poorest countries. As Christians, we are taught to care for the poor not to harm them. By utilizing greenhouse gas emitting energy options, we are inadvertently causing harm to millions of people in the poorest nations of the world. We can not live this way any longer - our energy usage must take into account children like Kara, so we can act as Christians do, giving a helping hand to those with less than us.

Notes: 1 Kings 17:8-16

The idea of people starving as the result of a famine is not an alien idea today, particularly as climate change continues to contribute to more extreme weather conditions in some of the most vulnerable countries of our world. But God’s compassion for the poor – even the poor who are not on our doorstep, who don’t share our nationality, is clear here.

Notes: Psalm 146

This is a song of praise – and what is it that drives the psalmist to praise? God the creator, and God the protector of the oppressed and poor, against those who work against them. God as creator and God as protector of the poor are clearly linked – both point to God as looking at the things God has made, and saying – I love this earth and its people. How can we look at what God has made and do the same?

Notes: Mark 12:38-44

Jesus is pretty stern in his words, here! What would a contemporary example of this kind of hypocrisy look like today? Maybe it would be proudly and openly donating to charity but choosing to look the other way when we hear shocking news stories about where our coffee or tea comes from, and the impact it has on local economies or environments. On the other hand, what it would like for us to give what we have? What gifts do we have that seem almost too small to give to God’s service?

Notes: 2 Samuel 23:1-7

Creation can give us pointers to how we can understand God. Here, living under God’s rule is like a beautiful, clear-skied, dewy morning.
