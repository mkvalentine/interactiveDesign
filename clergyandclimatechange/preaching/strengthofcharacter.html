Job 1:1; 2:1-10

There was once a man in the land of Uz whose name was Job. That man was blameless and upright, one who feared God and turned away from evil.

One day the heavenly beings came to present themselves before the LORD, and Satan also came among them to present himself before the LORD. The LORD said to Satan, "Where have you come from?" Satan answered the LORD, "From going to and fro on the earth, and from walking up and down on it." The LORD said to Satan, "Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one like him on the earth, a blameless and upright man who fears God and turns away from evil. He still persists in his integrity, although you incited me against him, to destroy him for no reason." Then Satan answered the LORD, "Skin for skin! All that people have they will give to save their lives. But stretch out your hand now and touch his bone and his flesh, and he will curse you to your face." The LORD said to Satan, "Very well, he is in your power; only spare his life."

So Satan went out from the presence of the LORD, and inflicted loathsome sores on Job from the sole of his foot to the crown of his head. Job took a potsherd with which to scrape himself, and sat among the ashes.

Then his wife said to him, "Do you still persist in your integrity? Curse God, and die." But he said to her, "You speak as any foolish woman would speak. Shall we receive the good at the hand of God, and not receive the bad?" In all this Job did not sin with his lips.

John 18:33-37

Then Pilate entered the headquarters again, summoned Jesus, and asked him, “Are you the King of the Jews?” 34 Jesus answered, “Do you ask this on your own, or did others tell you about me?” 35 Pilate replied, “I am not a Jew, am I? Your own nation and the chief priests have handed you over to me. What have you done?” 36 Jesus answered, “My kingdom is not from this world. If my kingdom were from this world, my followers would be fighting to keep me from being handed over to the Jews. But as it is, my kingdom is not from here.” 37 Pilate asked him, “So you are a king?” Jesus answered, “You say that I am a king. For this I was born, and for this I came into the world, to testify to the truth. Everyone who belongs to the truth listens to my voice.”

When confronted with any choice, we must weigh the pros and the cons; in many cases, this consists of weighing the positive benefits for one’s self over the negative consequences of an action that could come to others. There is no question that fossil fuels are a lucrative enterprise. Our global demand for these resources only continues to increase, making investment in such resources only continue to become more appealing from a financial perspective. However, as the financial benefits of these resources continue to rise, so does our understanding of their negative effects on our planet. Greater than 97% of all scientists believe that climate change is induced by human emissions of greenhouse gasses; that is an exceptionally high level of agreement for the scientific community. They also agree that at the current rate of emissions we could face catastrophic effects within this century. Fossil fuels are a business, and continued support of them only propagates their use. Here we are presented a choice, do we choose financially to support these organizations or do we look for alternative companies and resources that do not cause such negative harm in which we can invest? Individuals, corporations, organizations, universities, cities, and even religious groups are divesting from fossil fuels. The United Church of Christ has done so, along with many other religious organizations. These organizations have chosen to do so, knowing the potential financial costs for their groups, yet they made the choice because they knew that it was what is right. If we want to make choices that benefit ourselves without hurting others on an individual scale, why wouldn’t we make these choices on a macro-financial scale? Divestment from fossil fuels is one opportunity that we have as Christians to demonstrate our integrity, strength of character, and our ability to self-sacrifice.

Notes: Psalm 24

This is the psalm for the entrance to the temple: that, as one enters into the place of worship, the mind is turned to God’s rule of the earth and all its creatures, including humans. I wonder what our worship would look like today if we began it by looking out at the world and remembering that it does not belong to us?

Notes: Mark 12:38-44

Jesus is pretty stern in his words, here! What would a contemporary example of this kind of hypocrisy look like today? Maybe it would be proudly and openly donating to charity but choosing to look the other way when we hear shocking news stories about where our coffee or tea comes from, and the impact it has on local economies or environments. On the other hand, what it would like for us to give what we have? What gifts do we have that seem almost too small to give to God’s service?

Notes: John 18:33-37

The Kingdom of God is a countercultural kingdom. Jesus did not come to rule in a way which fit the norms and expectations of his people, his time, even his religion! How countercultural are we willing to be – and how much are we willing to sacrifice – to testify to the truth of who God is, and what we know to be true? We will probably never be threatened with death, but sometimes we are even afraid of making people feel uncomfortable by proposing counter-cultural ways of living. Will we take up the challenge?
