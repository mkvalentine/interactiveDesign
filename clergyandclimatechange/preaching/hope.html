Imagine a vacant lot in downtown New Haven, CT. during the day, it is only asphalt and weeds, during the night, it is a haven for drug dealers and violence. A few years ago, this decrepit lot was turned into a garden that provides fresh vegetables for the community that cultivates its growth. A group of concerned community members in New Haven decided to develop a program, New Haven Farms, where they planted gardens in areas that were once abandoned lots in the city of New Haven. Now, community members help to grow the gardens themselves, participate in nutrition education classes, and low-income workers are able to take home the vegetables that they farm themselves. It sounds almost too good to be true, doesn’t it, transforming this place of violence and sin into a place of peace, growth, and natural beauty? But that is what we can all do both inside ourselves, in our community, and in our planet. Inside us and around us we are able to find renewal, especially within the gifts of nature. As our planet is continuing to change, we must remember that it is not too late to make a difference. We can halt warming when we work together as a community, and we must see past the negative impacts into a hopeful better future as a Christians.

Notes: Isaiah 25:6-9

The vision Isaiah paints of the kingdom of God is one of life – in all its vibrancy, earthiness and diversity, without the shadow of pain and suffering that we live with now. It is a picture that speaks to the goodness of the world God has made – so good it is worth saving, in its wholeness!

Notes: Revelation 21:1-6a

On all saints day, as we remember those who have gone before, it is sometimes difficult to remember their lives without remembering the hardships and struggles they experienced, on this broken earth – but here is a picture of all things brought to completion, God’s people living in God’s presence. We who are still here have work to do, and sometimes it seems so overwhelming – but we have a sure promise of the future.

Notes: Ruth 3:1-5, 4:13-17

Ruth’s story is such an incredible insight into God’s ‘long-game’! Here is a woman from outside of the tribe of Israel, who obediently follows the odd advice of her mother-in-law in a strange land, amonst a foreign people, and she ends up becoming the great-grandmother of the King of Israel – the line leading to Jesus! When we think about some of the things we are called to do in service of others and God, they can seem difficult, even bizarre, but God has a ‘long-game’. Are we willing to take risks to be part of it?

Notes: Mark 13:1-8

Jesus talks surprisingly often in a way that seems to give creation ‘feelings’, even if in a metaphorical way. He talks about stones crying out, and here he describes the ‘birth pangs’ of the earth as they await the renewal of everything. I wonder how often we think about creation as having its own relationship to God, separate from our interaction with it? Certainly, the psalmist seems to talk that way!
